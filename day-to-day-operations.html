Day To Day Operations

<-- Back to A Severed Story Table of Contents

(Running the Workday)

This chapter is for the GM. It lays out the shape of a typical workday on the severed floor, then offers tables of small events to drop into one — for texture, or to seed a deliberate beat. Use as much or as little as your story needs.

Session vs. Workday

One note on terminology first, as the two are easy to confuse.

A session is a real-world meeting between you and your players — one sitting at the table. A workday is one in-game day on the severed floor, beginning with the elevator descent and ending with the elevator ascent.

One session may hold one workday, several workdays, or part of a single longer one. You decide how many workdays a session spans, in step with the pace of the story.

The Shape of a Workday

A typical workday at Lumon follows a rhythm of arrival, briefing, work, break, work, evaluation, and departure. Formalize whichever beats suit your campaign — run each in turn, or skip ahead to the moments that matter.

  • The Descent — the elevator door closes upstairs and opens downstairs. The innie wakes. A ritual moment, when it serves the story.

  • Morning Briefing — the team gathers. The Floor Manager (or their proxy) speaks. The day's quotas, expectations, or announcements are delivered.

  • Morning Work Block — the bulk of the day's productive work.

  • Mid-Day Break — the food bar opens. Employees gather, eat, and converse. A natural moment for inter-team beats.

  • Afternoon Work Block — a continuation of the day's work, or a new task introduced.

  • End-of-Day Evaluation — quotas are checked. Praise or concern is offered. The Floor Manager reviews the day.

  • The Ascent — the elevator returns. The innie sleeps.

The rhythm is not for your comfort. It is how they keep you compliant.

— C.C.

The Event Tables

Below are Lumon's sanctioned workday-event tables — five d20 tables of the small, pleasant occurrences that make a severed-floor day feel real. Roll a d20 for any table as needed, or simply pick the entry the moment calls for. They are arranged roughly in the order they fit the rhythm of a workday.

On Routine, and the Earning of Points

Kier Points are not earned from routine. The day-to-day flow of the workday should be played as the rhythm of life on the severed floor — not as a series of point-earning opportunities.

That said, sustained routine excellence is its own narrative beat. An employee who delivers an impeccable quarter — meeting quota each workday, never missing a beat — has earned the recognition of management. In such a moment, a Floor Manager may elevate the routine into a story beat and award a Kier Point, in line with the Earning Kier Points rules.

Points are awarded at the GM's discretion, in the moment.

Stress During the Workday

Stress is GM discretion. The events on these tables do not impose stress automatically. Add (or relieve) stress when the moment calls for it — see the Stress and Outcomes sections of Procedural Guidelines.

A long string of interruptions may justify a stress increase. A graceful navigation of a Management Visit may justify a reduction. You are the judge.

Dear reader,

Roll on mine when you want the players to feel the truth bleeding through the routine — or simply read them, and decide how much of it your table is ready for.

Table VI — What Actually Happens

The same workday. The parts they would never print.

  • 1. A coworker's chair is occupied by a stranger no one recognizes — and no one asks.

  • 2. Two regular coworkers are absent. No announcement is made. By afternoon, no one recalls them.

  • 3. A coworker is humming a song they could not possibly have learned here.

  • 4. A coworker begins crying at their desk and cannot say why.

  • 5. A coworker has bandaged their hand and does not remember when or how.

  • 6. The room smells of burnt coffee, though there is none on the floor.

  • 7. A workstation has moved three feet overnight. Everyone works as if it always sat there.

  • 8. The PA chimes. No announcement follows. An hour later, it chimes again.

  • 9. A coworker leaves the room and returns forty minutes later, calm, with no account of where they went.

  • 10. A coworker greets you by a name that is not yours, then corrects themselves too quickly.

  • 11. A coworker confides a dream — though dreams should not be possible here.

  • 12. A coworker stares at the elevator doors for ten minutes.

  • 13. A coworker reports another for something small. The reported coworker is not seen tomorrow.

  • 14. The bathroom is closed "for fumigation." It does not reopen.

  • 15. A coworker is led out and returns smiling faintly, and is not quite themselves after.

  • 16. The wall clock runs six minutes fast. By morning, the hallway clock has caught up to it.

  • 17. The elevator stops on a floor between known floors. The doors do not open. Something breathes on the other side.

  • 18. A Wellness "gift basket" is given to a coworker, who afterward will not speak of it.

  • 19. A coworker asks for help with something they cannot describe, and weeps when you cannot.

  • 20. A coworker has gone missing. No one else has noticed. Their desk is already reassigned.
