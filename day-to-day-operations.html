Day To Day Operations

<-- Back to A Severed Story Table of Contents

(The Rhythm of a Workday)

Every productive workday at Lumon Industries follows a familiar shape. New hires often find comfort in the predictability of our rhythms — the cadence of arrival, work, evaluation, and departure that defines life on a severed floor.

This section is offered to the GM as inspiration: a series of suggested beats that may shape any given workday, and a collection of events from which to draw when the moment calls for one.

Session vs. Workday

A note on terminology that will recur throughout this guide.

A session is a real-world meeting between the GM and the players — one sitting at the table. A workday is one in-game day on the severed floor, beginning with the elevator descent and ending with the elevator ascent.

One session may contain one workday, several workdays, or part of a longer workday. The GM decides how many workdays a session spans, in accordance with the pace of the story.

The Shape of a Workday

A typical workday at Lumon follows a rhythm of arrival, briefing, work, break, work, evaluation, and departure. GMs are encouraged to formalize whichever beats suit the campaign — running each in turn, or skipping ahead to the moments that matter.

  • The Descent — the elevator door closes upstairs and opens downstairs. The innie wakes. A ritual moment when it serves the story.

  • Morning Briefing — the team gathers. The Floor Manager (or their proxy) speaks. Today's quotas, expectations, or announcements are delivered.

  • Morning Work Block — the bulk of the day's productive work.

  • Mid-Day Break — the food bar opens. Employees gather, eat, and converse. A natural moment for inter-team beats.

  • Afternoon Work Block — a continuation of the day's work, or a new task introduced.

  • End-of-Day Evaluation — quotas are checked. Praise or concern is offered. The Floor Manager reviews the day.

  • The Ascent — the elevator returns. The innie sleeps.

The rhythm is not for your comfort. It is how they keep you compliant.

— C.C.

The Event Tables

What follows are six d20 tables of workday events. They are offered as inspiration when the GM wants seasoning, or to deliberately seed a story beat.

All tables use the same die that resolves every Roll. Roll a d20 for each table as needed, or simply pick the entry the moment calls for.

The tables are presented roughly in the order they fit the rhythm of a workday.

Table I — Morning Arrivals

What is different about today, before work begins.

  • 1. A new motivational poster has appeared overnight on a previously bare wall.

  • 2. A coworker's chair is occupied by a stranger no one recognizes.

  • 3. A handwritten note is taped to the inside of an employee's desk drawer.

  • 4. The light above one workstation flickers. Maintenance has been notified.

  • 5. The stress balls are gone. All of them. No explanation.

  • 6. A potted plant has appeared in the corner of the room.

  • 7. The room is unusually warm. The thermostat reads correctly.

  • 8. The air smells faintly of strong coffee, though no coffee is available here.

  • 9. A workstation has been moved three feet to the left.

  • 10. A meeting reminder appears on an employee's screen. No one else remembers scheduling it.

  • 11. A normally locked storage room is ajar by an inch.

  • 12. Two regular coworkers are absent today. No announcement is made.

  • 13. The wall clock is six minutes ahead of the one in the hallway.

  • 14. A small vase of fresh flowers sits on the Department Chief's desk.

  • 15. The hallway carpet has been replaced overnight.

  • 16. A new file folder has been left on an employee's desk. It bears their name.

  • 17. An unfamiliar memo board has appeared near the elevator.

  • 18. An employee's usual route to their station is closed for cleaning.

  • 19. A coworker greets an employee by a name that is not theirs, then corrects themselves immediately.

  • 20. Nothing is different. Praise Kier.

Table II — Routine Interruptions

Lumon-sanctioned events that pause the workday.

  • 1. A department-wide announcement asks everyone to remain seated and silent for ninety seconds.

  • 2. A Wellness Counselor wishes to speak briefly with a randomly selected employee.

  • 3. The lights flicker, then return.

  • 4. A safety drill: under the desk, count to thirty, resume work.

  • 5. A printer in another room reprints the same page repeatedly. The sound carries.

  • 6. A representative from Optics & Design arrives to measure a wall.

  • 7. A loose ceiling tile must be replaced. The work begins immediately.

  • 8. A coworker is asked to step out. They return ten minutes later, smiling faintly.

  • 9. The PA system chimes, but no announcement follows.

  • 10. Unfamiliar music is played overhead for the next eight minutes.

  • 11. The water cooler tastes faintly of pencils today.

  • 12. An emergency drill: everyone files into the hallway, returns three minutes later.

  • 13. A team-building exercise: each employee must say something they admire about a coworker.

  • 14. A surprise pop quiz on the Nine Core Principles.

  • 15. The break room is closed for fumigation. No timeline is given.

  • 16. An equipment audit. A clipboarded employee from Security counts staplers in silence.

  • 17. A motivational reading from The Compliance Handbook plays on a loop for ten minutes.

  • 18. A surprise gift card is awarded to a randomly selected employee.

  • 19. The Floor Manager will be unavailable for the next hour. Inquiries are not encouraged.

  • 20. A request: one employee must volunteer for an unspecified task.

Table III — Coworker Beats

What an NPC coworker is doing today. PC coworkers act on their own.

  • 1. A coworker is humming a song they cannot remember learning.

  • 2. A coworker quietly asks if you can keep a secret.

  • 3. A coworker has begun crying at their desk. They cannot explain why.

  • 4. A coworker is taking notes that are not work-related.

  • 5. A coworker has bandaged their hand. They do not remember how they did it.

  • 6. A coworker compliments your work with unusual specificity.

  • 7. A coworker offers you an item from their lunch they "felt you should have."

  • 8. A coworker is unusually quiet today. No one mentions it.

  • 9. A coworker is unusually loud today. The Floor Manager has been informed.

  • 10. A coworker repeats a phrase three times during a single conversation.

  • 11. A coworker leaves the room and does not return for forty minutes.

  • 12. A coworker asks you a personal question that should have no answer.

  • 13. A coworker spends ten minutes staring at the elevator doors.

  • 14. A coworker has begun a project no one assigned them.

  • 15. A coworker reports another coworker for something minor.

  • 16. A coworker confides a dream they had — though dreams should not be possible here.

  • 17. A coworker has begun mirroring another employee's mannerisms.

  • 18. A coworker insists you owe them a favor but cannot remember what for.

  • 19. A coworker quietly asks for help with something they cannot describe.

  • 20. A coworker has gone missing. No one else seems to have noticed.

Table IV — Management Visits

A visit from a higher floor.

  • 1. The Floor Manager observes the team for ten minutes, then leaves without comment.

  • 2. A representative from another department arrives to "compare notes."

  • 3. The Floor Manager calls a single employee aside for a private conversation.

  • 4. A senior figure visits to deliver a brief inspirational speech.

  • 5. An auditor from above arrives and requests production figures.

  • 6. A visiting manager wishes to administer a brief temperament evaluation.

  • 7. The Floor Manager hands out individually wrapped congratulatory items, one per employee.

  • 8. A surprise inspection of all employee LIEPs.

  • 9. A senior figure asks an employee to recite the Nine Core Principles from memory.

  • 10. A photographer from Optics & Design arrives to take a team portrait. Employees must smile.

  • 11. A team-mixing exercise: one employee is loaned to another department for the rest of the day.

  • 12. A representative from Wellness wishes to schedule sessions with each employee in turn.

  • 13. The Floor Manager asks who would be willing to stay late.

  • 14. A surprise quarterly review of an individual employee, conducted publicly.

  • 15. A senior figure conducts a tour of the floor with three unfamiliar guests.

  • 16. The Floor Manager asks an employee a question whose answer they should not know.

  • 17. A visiting manager praises one employee specifically, by name, and lingers.

  • 18. A senior figure asks each employee what they remember about yesterday.

  • 19. The Floor Manager delivers a Demerit to an employee, without explanation.

  • 20. An Eagan visits in person. All present are asked to applaud upon arrival.

Table V — Discoveries

Most discoveries are player-driven — the result of an employee searching, snooping, or asking. This table may be used as the result of such an effort, or to seed a discovery passively (an item left at a workstation overnight, a folded note found in a pocket).

  • 1. A handwritten note, signed only with initials.

  • 2. A coworker's name written on the back of a memo, followed by a question mark.

  • 3. A photograph of a stranger, expression unreadable.

  • 4. A child's drawing of a place that resembles the severed floor.

  • 5. A handwritten page torn from The You You Are.

  • 6. A keycard that does not belong to its holder.

  • 7. A grainy security camera printout showing an unfamiliar corridor.

  • 8. A scrap of fabric — soft, scented faintly of soap.

  • 9. A list of employee codes, half of which are crossed out.

  • 10. A schedule that does not match the floor's published one.

  • 11. A wrapped piece of hard candy, store-bought. (Impossible here.)

  • 12. A coworker's stress ball, hidden in an unusual place.

  • 13. A cassette tape labeled with a single word: REMEMBER.

  • 14. An incomplete, hand-annotated blueprint of the floor.

  • 15. A diary entry written in someone else's handwriting.

  • 16. A coworker's LIEP, with stress checkboxes filled to Burnout.

  • 17. A small mirror, palm-sized, edges worn.

  • 18. A copy of the Compliance Handbook with passages circled and underlined.

  • 19. A folded note bearing the employee's outie's name — in their own handwriting — that they cannot remember writing.

  • 20. A door key. No door is known to require it.

Table VI — End-of-Day Twists

A disruption to the routine departure.

  • 1. The elevator is delayed by ten minutes. No reason is given.

  • 2. A coworker is asked to remain after hours.

  • 3. A goodbye party for an employee who has reached Burnout.

  • 4. A new memo announces an unusually early arrival tomorrow.

  • 5. An employee is escorted out a side door by Security.

  • 6. The Floor Manager asks an employee to stay back for "a quick word."

  • 7. The elevator stops on a floor between known floors. The doors do not open.

  • 8. An employee finds a personal item in their pocket they cannot explain.

  • 9. The Floor Manager announces a Demerit assigned to an unnamed employee.

  • 10. The hallway lights flicker as the team walks toward the elevator.

  • 11. An employee asks to remain a moment longer. They are politely refused.

  • 12. A coworker is sent home with a "wellness gift basket" and does not speak when asked about it.

  • 13. A previously routine farewell ritual is altered. No explanation is given.

  • 14. The elevator is not where it should be. The team is sent up by a different route.

  • 15. An employee is selected for a brief "exit interview" that takes longer than expected.

  • 16. A Wellness Session is scheduled for tomorrow morning.

  • 17. The Floor Manager hands each employee a wrapped item to "open tomorrow."

  • 18. A senior figure rides the elevator up with the team in silence.

  • 19. The elevator stops; doors open onto a normally-locked space; doors close; the ride continues.

  • 20. The team is asked to walk silently down the corridor today. No reason is given.

On Routine, and the Earning of Points

Kier and Ricken Points are not earned from routine. The day-to-day flow of the workday should be played at the table as the rhythm of life on the severed floor — not as a series of point-earning opportunities.

That said, sustained routine excellence is its own narrative beat. An employee who delivers an impeccable quarter — meeting their quota each workday, never missing a beat — has earned the recognition of management. A Floor Manager may, in such a moment, choose to elevate the routine into a story moment and award a Kier Point in line with the Earning Points rules.

The same applies in reverse for Ricken Points. An employee who has spent many workdays quietly absorbing the patterns of the floor may, in the moment they finally see through them, earn a Point on the other Track.

Points are awarded at the GM's discretion, in the moment.

Stress During the Workday

Stress is GM discretion. The events on the tables above do not impose stress automatically. The GM may add (or relieve) stress when the moment calls for it — see the Stress and Outcomes sections of Procedural Guidelines.

A long string of Routine Interruptions may justify a stress increase. A successful navigation of a Management Visit may justify a stress reduction. The GM is the judge.

With the rhythm of the workday in hand, GMs may turn to Classified for the deeper toolkit — antagonist patterns, surveillance protocols, the secrets of Lumon, and the levers that drive a campaign forward.
