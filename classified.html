(Running the Game as the GM)

Dear GM,

What follows is not part of the orientation guide. It is a Lumon internal management manual — the kind given only to those who run a severed floor. I obtained a copy at no small cost, and have reproduced it here.

Players: stop reading. This chapter is the GM's. Knowing what follows will spoil the only thing Lumon ever truly gives its employees — the not-knowing.

GM: the manual's voice is Lumon's. Mine appears, as always, in the margins.

— C.C.

LUMON INDUSTRIES — RESTRICTED. This manual is the property of Lumon Industries and is intended solely for the use of authorized management personnel. The protocols, procedures, and provisions herein are not to be disclosed to severed-floor employees.

This chapter is the GM's toolkit: the mechanics of employee correction, the tracking of trust, the protocols that govern the Severance Chip, the people and places of the severed floor, and the events you may stage.

Everything here is yours to wield, ignore, or rewrite. You are the General Manager.

The Break Room

The Break Room is Lumon's instrument of correction. An employee sent to the Break Room is asked to read a statement of contrition — and to read it, and read it, until they mean it.

Mechanically, the Break Room is a tool the GM uses to challenge a player and push the story forward. What it costs the employee — and how much — is the GM's decision. It is reserved for serious transgressions: the kind of act that, were it discovered, Lumon could not let pass.

Suggested Break Room Effects

Choose one, combine several, or invent your own:

  • The employee gains a quantity of stress of the GM's choosing.

  • The employee rolls with Hesitance for the remainder of the current workday, and/or the whole of the next.

  • The strain of the room cracks something loose — the employee experiences a flash of outie memory (an Override / Bleed-Through moment; see Severance Chip Protocols).

  • The employee loses Kier Points, or the department loses Departmental Standing.

  • A lasting narrative consequence: a changed demeanor, a haunted look, a new fear the employee cannot explain.

The Break Room does not automatically trigger a Wellness Session. Whether one follows — and whether it offers any relief — is entirely up to the GM.

Use it rarely. The Break Room loses its teeth if it becomes routine. Its horror is in the anticipation — make the players dread the door.

— C.C.

Departmental Standing

The strength of a chain is measured by its weakest link. And so, Lumon does not trust its employees individually. It trusts — or distrusts — them as a department. A department's collective reputation is its Departmental Standing: a single rating, shared by the entire team, that determines how closely the floor is watched.

Departmental Standing is tracked on the LIEP, and is raised or lowered by the GM. There are five levels.

  • V — Exemplary. The department is a model for the company. Doors stand open. Cameras are few. The team moves with remarkable freedom.

  • IV — Commendable. A trusted department in good standing. Routine oversight only.

  • III — Satisfactory. Acceptable, unremarkable. Standard surveillance. The default for most departments.

  • II — Probationary. The department is under scrutiny. New cameras appear. Doors that were open are now locked.

  • I — Censured. The department is treated as a liability. The floor bristles with surveillance. Movement is restricted, privileges suspended, and every action is observed.

A department typically begins at IV — Commendable, enjoying the benefit of the doubt afforded to fresh hires. The GM may begin a campaign at any level the story demands.

Raising and Lowering Standing

When any member of the department is caught doing something untrustworthy in Lumon's eyes — or against Lumon's interests — the GM may drop Departmental Standing by one level. The crucial word is caught.

Standing rises more slowly: through sustained compliance, exemplary quarterly results, or a visible act of loyalty that restores the company's faith. The GM raises it when the department has earned it.

The players should feel their standing change through the fiction — not merely be told a number moved. A dropped level looks like a newly-installed camera dome, a keycard reader where none was before, a corridor that no longer opens. A raised level looks like a lock clicking open, a guard reassigned, a door left ajar.

Individual surveillance.

How closely any single employee is watched should be impacted by Departmental Standing, but further specifics are left to GM narration.

Severance Chip Protocols

The Severance Chip is more than a partition between innie and outie. It is a control surface. The following Protocols are documented overrides that authorized personnel may — under the proper conditions, from the proper terminals — initiate upon a severed employee.

The exact behavior of a Protocol is ultimately up to the GM. These are suggested story tools only.

  • Overtime Contingency. Activates an employee's innie in the outside world, granting the innie control of the body beyond the severed floor. Intended for emergencies; coveted by any innie who dreams of seeing the world above.

  • Glasgow Block. Keeps the innie dormant, ensuring an outie is in control regardless of location.

  • Clean Slate. A complete wipe of the innie. Everything they were — every memory, relationship, and shred of self formed on the floor — is erased. A fresh innie wakes in the same body, remembering nothing.

  • Goldfish. Disables the formation of new long-term memories while preserving basic function. The employee works, speaks, and moves — but each day, and sometimes each hour, slips away behind them.

  • Freeze Frame. Halts all motor function while leaving the mind awake. The employee is frozen in place, fully conscious, unable to move or speak.

  • Lullaby. Forces the innie to sleep. Considered risky — prolonged use is rumored to cause memory to leak between innie and outie — and so used sparingly, if at all.

  • Elephant. The inverse of a wipe: restores or preserves memory, and may open the door to reintegration of innie and outie. Profoundly dangerous to Lumon's order.

  • Beehive. Binds a group of severed employees into a shared, hive-like consciousness, allowing Lumon to direct the collective as one.

  • Open House. Permits more than one mind to inhabit a single body at once — a temporary, unstable coexistence of consciousnesses.

  • Branch Transfer. Relocates a severed employee's innie to a different Lumon branch, scrubbing confidential knowledge in transit so nothing travels with them.

This list is not exhaustive, and GMs are encouraged to develop new Protocols of their own.

A Protocol can be the seed of an entire campaign, or they can be left wholly unexplored. It's up to the GM how they are used.

Guard the terminals.

Protocols can only be initiated from secure terminals in restricted spaces — security offices, control rooms, the Testing Floor. As GM, take care that employees never gain unintended access to these rooms. A clever, determined party will try. The fear of what they might do with that access — and the lengths Lumon goes to prevent it — is a powerful engine for tension. Let reaching such a room be a hard-won, dangerous, climactic thing.

Antagonists of the Floor

The severed floor is populated by figures of authority — none of them, by design, fully knowable. The system is too light for full statblocks; instead, each archetype is described by how they think and how they pressure the employees. Run them as people with goals, not obstacles with hit points.

The Floor Manager

The face of Lumon on the floor. Warm and menacing in equal measure, the Floor Manager believes — genuinely — that they are helping. They reward compliance with theatrical generosity and punish defiance with wounded disappointment. They are always watching, and always smiling. Their pressure is intimacy: they want to be loved, and they make disappointing them feel like a personal failing.

The Wellness Counselor

Soft-spoken, soothing, and entirely opaque. The Wellness Counselor offers comfort that doubles as interrogation. In the Wellness Session, an employee is told facts about their outie — facts designed to please, to pacify, to bind them tighter to the company. Their pressure is seduction: they make the cage feel like care.

The Security Officer

Silent, watchful, immovable. The Security Officer rarely speaks and never explains. They appear when standing drops, when contraband surfaces, when an employee strays. Their pressure is force: the unspoken promise that some doors, once you pass through them, do not open again.

The Visiting Senior Figure

An executive from above, descending into the severed floor like weather. They speak in the cadence of scripture and treat the employees as both beloved children and laboratory subjects. Their visits are events. Their pressure is awe: they embody a hierarchy so vast and so certain that resistance feels not dangerous but absurd.

The Mysterious Outsider

Not all who walk the floor are who they claim. The Mysterious Outsider may be a planted spy, a defector from another department, an outie passing a message inward, or something stranger still. They are the GM's wildcard. Their pressure is uncertainty: the players can never be sure whose side they are on — including, sometimes, their own.

Known Departments

A severed floor is a constellation of departments, each doing their own integral and valuable work. The GM may place the players in any of them and populate the rest of the floor as the story requires. The departments below are those Lumon is known to operate on its severed floors.

  • Macrodata Refinement (MDR). Refiners sort vast fields of numbers into bins by the feelings the numbers evoke — sorting, in effect, by the Four Tempers. The work is mysterious and important. Blue badges.

  • Optics and Design (O&D). Keepers of Lumon's craft: storing and restoring the company's paintings, maintaining its color palettes and branding, and — behind a far door — producing the floor's many fine objects. Green badges.

  • Wellness (WN). Where the weary are restored. Counselors guide employees through Wellness Sessions — soft music, gentle words, and chosen facts about one's outie, all in the name of care.

  • Mammalians Nurturable (MN). Within a rolling indoor pasture of living grass, a large staff raises and tends goats, assessing each against the Core Principles, summoned by the ring of a bell.

  • Choreography and Merriment (C&M). Lumon's musicians: a marching band of brass, woodwind, and percussion that performs for ceremonies and celebrations, marching the corridors to honor a triumph.

  • Distribution Supply (DS). The source of the floor's goods. When a supply runs short, it is from here that a fresh one is fetched — some minutes' walk from any department.

  • Disposal and Reclamation (D&R). A department known chiefly by its name. What is disposed of, and what is reclaimed, Lumon does not say.

  • Janitorial and Hygiene (J&H). The department charged with keeping Lumon clean.

Departments seen but not understood.

Directories and security displays reference codes whose meanings Lumon has never made plain — among them CE, TA, W&A, EQP, and CL. Leave them as ambient mystery, or seize one as the seed of a story. An employee transferred to "TA" with no idea what the letters stand for is a fine way to begin an adventure.

Lumon names the departments it is proud of. But you should ask the questions this handbook never will. Who cuts the melon for the melon bar? Who irons the waffles? The corridors are not the same this week as last; a wall has moved. Who built that?

There are innies down here Lumon never celebrates, because their labor is meant to be invisible. I've set down the ones I'm certain must exist. You will think of more.

— C.C.

The following departments appear in no official Lumon material — they are reasoned inferences, the labor a severed floor must require whether or not the company admits it. Use them freely, place players within them, or invent others besides.

  • Food Services. The melon bars, the egg bars, the waffles, the coffee — someone prepares them and wheels them down. A culinary operation must hum somewhere beyond the corridors.

  • Facilities & Restoration. The corridors are not fixed — walls move, rooms are repurposed, the Perpetuity Wing gleams as new. A staff of builders and restorers must shape and reshape the maze.

  • Horticulture & Grounds. The hallway plants are real; the goats graze on living grass. Something green is grown and tended down here, far from any window or sun — and someone tends it.

An unseen department makes an excellent home for a player who wants to be somewhere stranger than MDR — or a destination for a Ricken-aligned employee following the supply lines, the trash, or the night shift toward a truth Lumon would rather keep buried.

Spaces of the Severed Floor

The floor is a maze, and the maze is the point. Below are spaces the GM may build into their severed floor, each with a note on its use at the table. GM's are additionally encouraged to develop more spaces beyond these listed.

  • The Elevator & Stairwell. The threshold between innie and outie.

  • The Department Floor. The employees' home. The work itself. The default stage for most scenes. This space may look vastly different based on what it is the department actually does.

  • The Break Room. Correction made physical. See above. Players should learn to fear its mention.

  • The Wellness Center. Where employees are soothed, rewarded, and quietly studied. Calm lighting, soft music, total surveillance.

  • The Perpetuity Wing. A museum to the Eagan dynasty — part shrine, part propaganda, part mausoleum. A place to deliver lore, and to feel watched by the dead.

  • The Corridors. Endless, identical, white. Easy to get lost in; easy to hide in; easy to be found in. The maze between every other space.

  • The Floor Manager's Office. Where praise and punishment are delivered in private. Being summoned here is never neutral.

  • Storage / Supply Closets. Mundane, overlooked, and therefore perfect — for hiding contraband, for stolen conversations, for finding what was not meant to be found.

  • Conference Rooms. The stage for meetings, for interrogations, for secret conversations.

  • Unused Space. Whether its unused hallways kept dimly lit to preserve electricity, or unused offices resulted from corporate restructuring, a severed floor always consists of these more unsettling spaces near the edges.

  • The Testing Floor. A restricted zone. Secure terminals. Protocols. The deep secret of the campaign likely lives here. Reaching it should be climactic.

Corporate Events

Lumon punctuates the grind with rituals of reward and control. Each serves a different purpose at the table — so these are offered as a menu to stage deliberately, rather than a table to roll on.

  • The Melon Bar. A spread of cut fruit wheeled out to reward a productive stretch — Lumon's small, strange generosity made edible. A low-stakes gathering: the social hub of the workday, a natural stage for coworker beats, and a quiet chance to pass a word (or an object) to a neighbor.

  • The Egg Bar. A more lavish cousin of the Melon Bar — eggs prepared to order, presented with unsettling ceremony. Reserved for a notable achievement. The grandeur is the point: a reward generous enough to make the employees wonder what, exactly, they have done to deserve it.

  • The Waffle Party. The highest honor a department can earn — lavish, strange, and faintly ceremonial. Dangle it as a goal; deliver it as a reward that unsettles as much as it delights.

  • The Music Dance Experience. The Floor Manager wheels out a small music player and offers the team a moment of sanctioned joy. A scene of forced, supervised release — comedic and bleak in the same breath.

  • The Quarterly Review. A formal accounting of the department's performance. A natural moment to adjust Departmental Standing, award Kier Points, or stage a public reckoning.

  • The Team Mixer. Two departments forced to socialize. Tension, comedy, and the rare chance to make a forbidden interdepartmental connection (a Ricken opportunity).

  • The Wellness Fair. A floor-wide event of mandatory relaxation. Booths, exercises, and gentle coercion. Pleasant on the surface.

  • The Retirement Party. When an employee is Terminated or transferred, Lumon stages a send-off of unsettling cheer. A goodbye for someone who will not remember leaving.

That is the manual. The rest is yours. Run it with cruelty or with mercy, for laughs or for chills — but run it well, and remember the employees in your care are people, even the ones who only live nine floors down and nine hours a day.
