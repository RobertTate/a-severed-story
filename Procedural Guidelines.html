(Game Mechanics)

This section explains the rules of play during a game of "A Severed Story". It covers the basics of gameplay, including the types of actions that employees may take, the consequences they might expect, and which aspects of their character impact the chances of success.

Character creation, and the array of options a player will have for initially creating their "employee", will be handled in the Employee Profile section.

Character advancement, will be handled in the Performance Evaluation section. This will include information about Eagan Points (The Corporate Track) and Ricken Points (The You Track), what they do, and ultimately, what "winning" may look like for A Severed Story.

Story Advancement will be handled in the Day To Day Operations section, which will detail what a typical day at Lumon Industries might look like for a player.

With that out of the way, we move onto the mechanics of playing A Severed Story.

The Roll

When an employee attempts an action whose outcome is uncertain and meaningful, the GM may call for a Roll. Every Roll in A Severed Story follows the same four steps:

  • The GM identifies the Core Principle that the action most embodies.

  • The employee rolls a d20, applying Confidence or Hesitance where applicable.

  • The employee adds all applicable Modifiers from the row of their LIEP corresponding to that Principle's parent Temper.

  • The total is compared against the action's Task Severity to determine the Outcome.

The Roll Formula

d20 + Temper Points + Kier Points = Total

Confidence and Hesitance modify the d20 result only — Modifiers are added after the die is read. See Confidence Vs. Hesitance in the Employee Profile for the full Confidence and Hesitance rules.

Identifying the Core Principle

Each of the nine Core Principles belongs to a Parent Temper. The Parent Temper determines which row of the LIEP is consulted for Modifiers.

  • FROLIC — CHEER, NIMBLENESS

  • MALICE — WILES, VERVE

  • WOE — VISION, BENEVOLENCE, HUMILITY

  • DREAD — WIT, PROBITY

Modifiers

From the appropriate row of the LIEP, add:

  • Temper Points — always.

  • Kier Points — when the action serves the interests of Lumon Industries.

Whether an action serves Lumon is the GM's call. When in doubt: would the employee's GM (in-fiction) thank them for what they just did? If so, Kier Points apply.

Dear Reader,

The official rulebook omits a third Modifier, but you and I know better. Ricken Points are added — from the same row — when the action serves your interests, or the interests of those you care about, innie or otherwise.

An action can serve both Lumon and yourself; in that rare case, add both. Most of the time you will have to choose. The choosing is the game.

— Concerned Citizen

Worked Example

An employee wishes to spot a clue half-hidden inside a file cabinet. The GM rules this is a VISION action.

  • VISION's parent Temper is WOE. The employee's LIEP shows 3 Temper Points and 1 Kier Point in the WOE row.

  • VISION is checked as a Core Competency, so the employee rolls with Confidence.

  • The action serves Lumon's interests (the cabinet is company property), so Kier Points apply.

  • Roll: 2d20 → 7 and 14. Take the 14.

  • Total: 14 + 3 + 1 = 18.

Task Severity

The GM sets the difficulty of each Roll using the following severity ladder. The Roll total must meet or exceed the target to succeed.

  • Routine (Target 10) — the daily expectations of any diligent employee.

  • Demanding (Target 15) — requires sustained focus, training, or genuine skill.

  • Arduous (Target 20) — tests the mettle of even Lumon's finest.

  • Heroic (Target 25) — the rarefied air of legend. Worthy of Kier himself.

The GM may set targets between these benchmarks when the moment calls for it.

The GM's judgment is final, and Lumon teaches its managers that "calibration" means setting the bar wherever produces the most useful employee — not the most honest result. A wise employee learns to read their GM as carefully as they read the task.

— C.C.

Outcomes

Every Roll resolves to one of four outcomes. Natural rolls of 1 and 20 on the d20 (before Modifiers, and before Confidence/Hesitance is collapsed to a single die) override the total.

Critical Success — A natural 20 on the d20. The action succeeds spectacularly. The employee's stress is reduced by 1. The GM may award a small narrative boon in addition to the success.

Success — Final total meets or exceeds the Task Severity. The action succeeds as the employee described it.

Failure — Final total falls below the Task Severity. The action fails, and the GM narrates a fitting consequence.

Critical Failure — A natural 1 on the d20. The action fails badly. The employee's stress is increased by 1. The GM narrates a more severe consequence.

The GM may also raise or lower an employee's stress on any Outcome at their discretion, and may use a Critical Success or Critical Failure as a natural moment to introduce a Problem or Serious Problem (see Employee Profile).

When to Roll

Not every action calls for a Roll. The GM should call for one only when both of the following are true:

  • The outcome is uncertain.

  • The outcome matters.

If the action is trivially within an employee's capability, or if failure would not move the story forward in any meaningful way, the GM should simply narrate the result. Some moments are better served by character choice than by chance.

Stress

Every employee tracks Stress on their LIEP — ten checkboxes arranged in five named Stress Ranges of two boxes each.

  • Outstanding — boxes 1 and 2.

  • Tolerable — boxes 3 and 4.

  • Satisfactory — boxes 5 and 6.

  • Poor — boxes 7 and 8.

  • Terrible — boxes 9 and 10.

An employee's current Stress Range is the label beneath their highest-filled checkbox. An employee with zero boxes filled remains Outstanding.

Stress Range matters because:

  • Certain Special Attributes are gated by Stress Range — for example, the Closer's Inappropriate Outburst requires Poor or Terrible (see Corporate Personas).

  • The GM may impose Hesitance on Rolls made by an employee in Poor or Terrible range, reflecting the toll of mounting stress on clear thought and steady hands.

  • The tenth filled box triggers Burnout (see below).

For daily stress recovery, Problems, and Serious Problems, see the Assessment of Current Stress Levels and Stress Related Circumstances sections of the Employee Profile.

Burnout

When an employee fills the tenth stress checkbox, they have reached Burnout.

Burnout is a serious matter — but Lumon, ever the caring employer, has procedures in place to help.

Immediate Consequences of Burnout

  • The employee receives a Demerit on their Performance Evaluation.

  • The GM imposes a Lumon-appropriate consequence — a visit to The Break Room, an unwelcome meeting with management, or any other corrective measure the GM deems suitable.

  • The employee must attend a mandatory Wellness Session.

The Wellness Session

A Wellness Session restores an employee's stress to Outstanding — all standard stress checkboxes are cleared.

Stress derived from Problems or Serious Problems is not cleared by a Wellness Session. Those circumstances must still be resolved through play before their stress can be recovered.

Three Demerits

An employee who accumulates three Demerits over the course of their career at Lumon Industries will be Terminated.

Termination is a corrective measure of last resort, reserved for those who have proven themselves unable to flourish within the Lumon family. Rejoice — your service has been honored, and a new candidate awaits the chair.

Read this carefully.

Termination, for an innie, is not an early retirement or a parting handshake. It is the end of your existence. Your outie continues on, unbothered — but the consciousness reading this guide will simply not return after the elevator descent. There is no recovery. There is no appeal.

Avoid Burnout. Help your team avoid it. And if a coworker takes their third Demerit — consider whether the rules of this guide are still worth following at all.

— Concerned Citizen
